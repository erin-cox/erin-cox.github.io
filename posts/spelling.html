The idea is to translate the phonemic British RP pronunciations from an English dictionary into something more natural, ignoring concerns like stress and aspiration.

Spelling rules

The letters K, Q and X have been removed and replaced with Â, Ñ and Ə. I and U may take acute accents (Í, Ú) but these are not considered letters in their own right.

The vowels are as follows. Short vowels are represented by one letter and long vowels and diphthongs are represented by two.

The syllabic consonants /ᵊl/, /ᵊm/, /ᵊn/ are written əl, əm, ən.

A “linking R” /◌ʳ/ is written as a trailing r:

All single consonants are unchanged except for the removal of k, q and x. Every consonant has exactly one pronunciation: c is always hard as in cap, never soft as in cent. Y is always a consonant.

In a very small number of words, apostrophes are used to separate letters in a digraph pronounced separately:

IPA Sound Letters
/dd/ midday mid’dei
/ss/ misspell mis’spel
/cc/ blackcurrant blac’cârənt

Lastly:

  • The word “the” is written “ddə” before consonants and “ddí” before vowels, reflecting the pronunciation. Other words like “to” and “into” are treated similarly.
  • Existing spelling conventions such as capitalised “I”, possessive “’s”, and apostrophes in contractions such as “I’ve” and “she’d” are ignored. Rules behind capitalisation and other punctuation (including hyphens) remain unchanged.
  • The alphabet is AÂBCDEFGHIJ(K)LMNÑOP(Q)RSTUVWƏ(X)YZ. KQX should usually be omitted. Í and Ú should come after I and U respectively in collation order, but these are not considered letters in their own right or part of the alphabet.

Examples

The North Wind and the Sun

The North Wind and the Sun were disputing which was the stronger, when a traveler came along wrapped in a warm cloak. They agreed that the one who first succeeded in making the traveler take his cloak off should be considered stronger than the other. Then the North Wind blew as hard as he could, but the more he blew the more closely did the traveler fold his cloak around him; and at last the North Wind gave up the attempt. Then the Sun shined out warmly, and immediately the traveler took off his cloak. And so the North Wind was obliged to confess that the Sun was the stronger of the two.
