--- title: A realistic proposal for English spelling reform (v0.2.0) layout: default ---

The idea is to take phonemic British RP pronunciations from an English dictionary and translate the IPA symbols into something more natural, ignoring stress and aspiration.

Letters

There are 13 basic vowels and 7 double vowels. Some of the vowels may sound different depending on your dialect; for example, I speak a dialect which merges u/w and wu/ó. (The use of w to denote the vowels in “foot” and “goose” is taken from Welsh.)

  • Accented letters (áéíóẃý) are not considered part of the alphabet.
  • The alphabet is as before but with kqx removed.
  • Every letter corresponds to exactly one sound (unless in a digraph).

With a few exceptions, most of the consonants remain unchanged. Every consonant has exactly one pronunciation: c is always hard as in cap, never soft as in cent. Note the distinction between th and dh.

The letters k, q and x are not used.

Apostrophes are used to separate letters in a digraph pronounced separately:

Standard New
boathouse bywt’haws
cowherd caw’hýd
mishap mis’hap
drawing dró’ing

Syllabic consonants are written with a y before the consonant:

Standard New
mansion manshyn
dazzle dazyl
chasm cazym
Lastly:
  • The word “the” is written “dhy” before consonants and “dhí” before vowels, reflecting the pronunciation.
  • Existing spelling conventions such as capitalised “I”, possessive “’s”, and apostrophes in contractions such as “I’ve” and “she’d” are ignored.
  • Rules behind capitalisation and other punctuation remain unchanged.

The North Wind and the Sun

The North Wind and the Sun were disputing which was the stronger, when a traveler came along wrapped in a warm cloak. They agreed that the one who first succeeded in making the traveler take his cloak off should be considered stronger than the other. Then the North Wind blew as hard as he could, but the more he blew the more closely did the traveler fold his cloak around him; and at last the North Wind gave up the attempt. Then the Sun shined out warmly, and immediately the traveler took off his cloak. And so the North Wind was obliged to confess that the Sun was the stronger of the two.

Practical usage

No new glyphs are introduced. The accented characters required are Áá Éé Íí Óó Ẃẃ Ýý, all of which can be found on existing Welsh keyboard layouts and in many standard fonts.

Capitalisation and punctuation standards remain unchanged.

Given the prior existence of a phonemic dictionary (the OED) and the regular nature of the spelling, this proposal is highly amenable to automation, with a simple dictionary implementation able to handle all but a small number of homographs such as “lead”, “close” and “wind”.
